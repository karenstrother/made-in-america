The new Made in America initiative is the result of a [Presidential Executive Order (E.O.) signed Jan. 25, 2021.](https://www.whitehouse.gov/briefing-room/presidential-actions/2021/01/25/executive-order-on-ensuring-the-future-is-made-in-all-of-america-by-all-of-americas-workers/){:target="_blank" rel="noreferrer" class="usa-link--external"} The E.O. created the Office of Management and Budget’s (OMB) Made in America Office (MIAO) and established a requirement for agencies to submit proposed waivers to the MIAO for review. The E.O. also directed the creation of this new website for public transparency. OMB subsequently issued guidance on [how to implement the Made in America E.O.](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/M-21-26.pdf){:target="_blank" rel="noreferrer" class="usa-link--external"} Regulatory actions related to this E.O. are publicly available at https://www.acquisition.gov/content/far-rule-published-and-open-comment.

With $600 billion in annual procurement spending, almost half of which is in manufactured products, from helicopter blades to trucks to medicine, the U.S. Government is a major buyer in a number of markets for goods and services, including the single largest purchaser of consumer goods in the world. Ensuring clear, consistent, and transparent implementation of Buy American policies will help the U.S. government leverage its purchasing power more effectively to advance the competitiveness of U.S. industries.

Strengthening Made in America policies will send clear market signals to give suppliers confidence that manufacturing in the U.S., with America’s workers, will provide greater opportunities. Ensuring that Made in America laws are implemented clearly and consistently across the whole government will help support domestic suppliers in the six critical supply chains identified by President Biden.

No. What’s the difference? The Buy American Act (BAA) is the primary law that requires the Federal government to prefer domestic goods and manufactured products when making purchasing decisions. The BAA and implementing regulations in the Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) provide a two-part test for determining if a product qualifies as a domestic end product: (i) the item must be manufactured in the United States, and (ii) more than 55 percent of the cost of all the component parts must be manufactured in the United States. The FAR provides various waivers and exceptions to the BAA. By contrast, Buy America Laws are applicable to Federal Financial Assistance (such as grants), not procurement. There is no single “Buy America” statute. Rather, there are a number of statutes that require those receiving Federal assistance to prefer goods, products, and materials made in the United States. For example, the Federal Transit Administration’s grant-making authority requires that the steel, iron, and manufactured goods used in the project are produced in the United States.

The 55 percent threshold is currently in effect, but on July, 30, 2021, the FAR Council proposed to increase the content threshold requirement immediately to 60 percent, and to further raise it to 75 percent over the next several years. Increasing the content thresholds is intended to strengthen the government’s commitment to American manufacturing and make Buy American real. If adopted, the higher thresholds will take effect in accordance with timeframes set forth in the final rule. Read more about this proposed rule here.
